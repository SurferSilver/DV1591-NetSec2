\begin{abstract}
This report examines emerging trends in the weaponization of artificial intelligence tools by threat actors. Through analysis of three case studies—PROMPTSTEAL, QUIETVAULT, and PROMPTFLUX—we document how both state-sponsored and financially motivated adversaries are leveraging large language models (LLMs) for malware development, evasion, and data exfiltration. The analysis draws from observations by Google Cloud Threat Intelligence, CERT-UA, and SentinelOne Labs, revealing a significant shift in how adversaries integrate generative AI into their operational tradecraft.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The integration of artificial intelligence into cybersecurity threats represents a significant evolution in the threat landscape. As large language models have become increasingly accessible through both cloud APIs and locally deployable solutions, threat actors have begun incorporating these tools into their operational workflows in ways that demand new defensive approaches.

Google Cloud Threat Intelligence has documented a notable influx of AI-related malware activity targeting their LLM services. Their observations reveal that adversaries are exploiting generative AI across the full spectrum of the attack lifecycle—from initial reconnaissance and social engineering through payload development and post-compromise data analysis. This trend is not limited to sophisticated state-sponsored actors; financially motivated cybercriminals are also adopting these techniques, lowering the barrier to entry for advanced attack capabilities.

Several key patterns have emerged from recent threat intelligence. First, malware authors are implementing ``just-in-time'' AI usage, where malicious code queries LLM APIs at runtime to dynamically generate commands or obfuscate payloads. This approach, exemplified by the PROMPTFLUX and PROMPTSTEAL campaigns, allows malware to evade static signature detection by producing unique code variants on each execution. Second, threat actors have developed social engineering techniques specifically designed to bypass AI safety guardrails, often framing malicious requests as educational exercises or legitimate development tasks. Third, illicit marketplaces have shown increased availability of AI-powered ransomware tools, suggesting that these techniques are proliferating within the criminal underground. Finally, state-sponsored actors—particularly APT28 (associated with Russian military intelligence) and various North Korean groups—continue to refine their use of AI tools for espionage and financial theft operations.

This report provides detailed analysis of three case studies that illustrate these trends: PROMPTSTEAL, a state-sponsored campaign leveraging cloud LLMs for reconnaissance; QUIETVAULT, a credential stealer that exploits locally hosted AI tools; and PROMPTFLUX, an experimental malware family demonstrating AI-powered polymorphic capabilities.

\section{Case Study 1: PROMPTSTEAL}

\subsection{LAMEHUG Technical Analysis}

LAMEHUG represents a significant evolution in how state-sponsored actors integrate artificial intelligence into their malware operations. Developed in Python, this malicious program demonstrates a novel approach to command generation by leveraging the Qwen 2.5-Coder-32B-Instruct large language model through the Hugging Face API at \url{https://huggingface.co}.

The malware's operational concept centers on ``just-in-time'' command generation. Rather than hardcoding specific reconnaissance commands—which would create static signatures detectable by security tools—LAMEHUG stores natural language descriptions of desired actions. At runtime, these descriptions are submitted to the cloud-hosted LLM, which generates the corresponding shell commands for execution on the victim system. This approach provides operational flexibility, as the same malware can adapt its behavior across different system configurations without requiring code modifications.

The reconnaissance capabilities implemented through this technique are comprehensive. LAMEHUG collects and stores basic system information in the file \texttt{\%PROGRAMDATA\%\textbackslash info\textbackslash info.txt}, including hardware specifications, running processes, active services, and network connections. The malware also performs recursive searches for documents of intelligence value—specifically Microsoft Office files, PDFs, and plain text documents—within the user's Documents, Downloads, and Desktop directories. Identified files are copied to a staging folder at \texttt{\%PROGRAMDATA\%\textbackslash info\textbackslash} for subsequent exfiltration.

Data exfiltration is accomplished through either SFTP connections or HTTP POST requests, depending on the malware variant. The collected information undergoes encoding before transmission to adversary-controlled infrastructure, providing a degree of obfuscation for the exfiltrated data.

A particularly noteworthy aspect of LAMEHUG is the social engineering applied to the LLM interactions themselves. The prompts submitted to Hugging Face were crafted to resemble Capture The Flag (CTF) competition exercises or student homework assignments. This framing exploits the tendency of AI safety systems to permit educational or learning-oriented requests, effectively bypassing guardrails designed to prevent malicious use.

\subsection{Target Profile and Campaign Context}

The PROMPTSTEAL campaign has been observed primarily targeting Ukrainian organizations, consistent with the broader pattern of Russian cyber operations against Ukraine. The malware is designed specifically for Windows environments, reflecting the prevalence of this platform in target organizations.

The campaign's objectives align with traditional espionage priorities: document theft and system reconnaissance. By automating the identification and collection of potentially sensitive documents, the attackers can efficiently gather intelligence materials for subsequent analysis, likely by human analysts rather than automated systems.

\subsection{Attribution}

CERT-UA reported the LAMEHUG malware in June 2025, attributing the activity to APT28 with moderate confidence. APT28, also known as Fancy Bear, Sofacy, and Pawn Storm, is a threat actor associated with Russia's Main Intelligence Directorate (GRU). The group has a well-documented history of conducting cyber espionage operations against government, military, and critical infrastructure targets, particularly in countries perceived as adversarial to Russian interests.

The attribution is based on technical indicators, targeting patterns, and operational tradecraft consistent with previous APT28 campaigns. However, the moderate confidence level reflects the inherent challenges in definitively attributing cyber operations, particularly when adversaries actively employ deception techniques.

\subsection{Indicators of Compromise}

Defenders should monitor for several indicators associated with PROMPTSTEAL operations. Initial access typically occurs through spear-phishing emails containing malicious attachments. Observed filename patterns include ``Appendix.pdf.zip'' and ``AI-generator-uncensored-Canvas-PRO-v-v0.9.exe''—the latter apparently designed to appeal to users interested in AI image generation tools.

Network-based indicators include API calls to Hugging Face services originating from hosts that should not legitimately require such access. Host-based indicators include the creation of the staging directory at \texttt{C:\textbackslash ProgramData\textbackslash info}, the presence of dynamically generated shell command execution patterns, and encoded data being prepared for exfiltration.

\subsection{Defensive Recommendations}

Effective defense against PROMPTSTEAL and similar threats requires a multi-layered approach. Network defenders should implement outgoing traffic filtering that specifically inspects and controls API traffic to AI service providers. While blocking all such traffic may not be feasible in organizations that legitimately use these services, anomalous access patterns from unexpected hosts should trigger alerts.

Endpoint security solutions should incorporate behavioral detection capabilities that can identify command execution chains characteristic of LLM-generated reconnaissance activity. Traditional signature-based detection is unlikely to be effective against dynamically generated commands, making behavioral analysis essential.

File system monitoring should be configured to detect bulk document copying operations, particularly when documents are being staged to unusual locations. Finally, organizations should maintain strict credential hygiene practices, as stolen API tokens could enable attackers to query LLM services without deploying infrastructure that might be detected.

\section{Case Study 2: QUIETVAULT}

\subsection{Technical Overview}

QUIETVAULT represents an innovative approach to credential theft that exploits the growing deployment of locally hosted AI tools. Unlike PROMPTSTEAL's reliance on cloud-based LLM services, QUIETVAULT targets systems where users have installed local AI inference capabilities, particularly the OLLAMA framework, turning the victim's own AI tools against them.

The malware is implemented in JavaScript and targets macOS and Linux systems—platforms where developer adoption of local AI tools has been particularly strong. This targeting reflects the reality that users interested in running local LLMs tend to be developers and technically sophisticated individuals, who often have access to valuable credentials, API tokens, and in many cases, cryptocurrency wallets.

\subsection{Target Profile}

QUIETVAULT operations focus on individual users and developers rather than enterprise targets. The malware specifically seeks systems with locally installed AI and LLM command-line tools, recognizing that such installations indicate a technically sophisticated user likely to possess valuable credentials.

Primary targets include cryptocurrency wallets and their associated seed phrases or private keys, authentication credentials stored in configuration files, API tokens for cloud services, and SSH keys and other authentication materials. The malware focuses on home directories and user-level configuration paths where such sensitive materials are commonly stored.

\subsection{Attribution and Motivation}

No public attribution to a named threat actor has been established for QUIETVAULT. Google Threat Intelligence, which first documented the malware, noted that similar variants likely exist on other platforms. The operational focus on cryptocurrency theft and credential harvesting strongly suggests financially motivated cybercrime rather than state-sponsored espionage.

\subsection{Attack Methodology}

QUIETVAULT's attack methodology demonstrates sophisticated understanding of how local AI tools can be weaponized. Upon execution, the malware first enumerates the system to identify locally installed LLM tools, with particular focus on OLLAMA installations.

Once a local LLM is identified, QUIETVAULT injects a carefully crafted natural language prompt into the AI tool. This prompt instructs the LLM to reason over filesystem contents and identify files likely to contain cryptocurrency wallet information, passwords, authentication tokens, and similar sensitive data. Critically, the prompt includes instructions to avoid privileged system directories, ensuring the reconnaissance remains within user-accessible paths and does not trigger security mechanisms monitoring for privilege escalation.

This approach provides several advantages to the attacker. The reconnaissance is performed by legitimate software already present on the system, reducing the behavioral footprint of the malware itself. The LLM's reasoning capabilities enable identification of sensitive files based on content patterns and naming conventions, rather than requiring hardcoded file paths. Additionally, because the AI analysis occurs locally, no network traffic is generated during this phase—the malware only needs to communicate externally during the final exfiltration stage.

\subsection{Exfiltration Techniques}

After the local LLM identifies files of interest, QUIETVAULT stages the data for exfiltration using Base64 encoding. The malware then employs a creative exfiltration channel: it uses locally stored GitHub credentials to create new repositories and upload the stolen data. This technique leverages legitimate services and the victim's own authentication credentials, making the traffic appear normal and bypassing many network security controls.

Indicators of QUIETVAULT compromise include unexpected invocation of local LLM CLI tools, recursive filesystem enumeration patterns originating from user context, Base64-encoded data being staged in temporary locations, and the creation of new GitHub repositories without user initiation.

\subsection{Defensive Recommendations}

Defending against QUIETVAULT requires attention to the security implications of locally deployed AI tools. Organizations and individuals should monitor the execution of local AI and LLM tooling, establishing baselines for normal usage patterns and alerting on anomalies.

Access controls should be implemented to restrict what filesystem paths AI CLI tools can access, limiting the potential for malicious reconnaissance even if prompt injection occurs. GitHub and similar services should be configured to require multi-factor authentication for repository creation, preventing attackers from silently creating exfiltration channels.

Endpoint detection solutions should be updated to recognize AI-assisted reconnaissance behaviors, including the characteristic patterns of LLM-guided file enumeration and the staging of encoded data for exfiltration.

\section{Case Study 3: PROMPTFLUX}

\subsection{Technical Overview}

PROMPTFLUX represents perhaps the most concerning development in AI-enabled malware: the use of generative AI for automated polymorphism. This experimental dropper malware, implemented in VBScript, queries the Google Gemini API to continuously modify its own source code, producing functionally equivalent but syntactically distinct variants that evade signature-based detection.

The core innovation lies in the ``StartThinkingRobot'' function, which interfaces with the Gemini API to request code modifications. Rather than using predefined mutation rules—as traditional polymorphic malware does—PROMPTFLUX leverages the LLM's understanding of code semantics to generate natural, varied transformations that maintain functionality while altering all surface-level indicators.

\subsection{Operational Implications}

Google has documented variations of PROMPTFLUX that rewrite the entire malware source code on an hourly basis. This frequency of mutation far exceeds what traditional polymorphic engines can achieve while maintaining code quality, and it presents significant challenges for defenders.

Traditional signature-based detection relies on identifying static patterns within malicious code. When malware can regenerate itself continuously using AI-guided transformations, no such stable patterns exist. Each instance of the malware is effectively unique, requiring security tools to shift entirely to behavioral detection approaches.

The implications extend beyond individual malware samples. If PROMPTFLUX-style techniques become widespread, the fundamental economics of malware detection shift in favor of attackers. Generating new, undetected variants becomes trivially cheap, while defenders face the computational burden of behavioral analysis for every potential threat.

\section{Conclusion}

The three case studies examined in this report collectively demonstrate that the integration of artificial intelligence into malicious operations represents a fundamental shift in the threat landscape rather than an incremental evolution. Each case study reveals different dimensions of this transformation, and together they paint a picture of adversaries who are rapidly adapting their tradecraft to exploit the capabilities that generative AI provides.

Google Cloud Threat Intelligence has documented a significant influx of AI-related malware targeting their LLM services, indicating that the experimental techniques observed in controlled research settings have transitioned into active operational use. The barrier between proof-of-concept and deployed threat has effectively collapsed for AI-enabled attack techniques.

The integration of cloud-based LLMs into malware operations, as demonstrated by PROMPTSTEAL, enables capabilities that were previously difficult or impractical to implement. Dynamic command generation allows malware to adapt to diverse target environments without requiring extensive pre-deployment testing. Reconnaissance that previously required hardcoded scripts or human operators can now be accomplished through natural language prompts, allowing attackers to specify intent rather than implementation details.

PROMPTFLUX's generative obfuscation capabilities represent perhaps the most significant challenge to traditional defensive approaches. When malware can regenerate itself hourly using AI-guided transformations, signature-based detection becomes fundamentally ineffective. The economics of the attacker-defender relationship shift decisively: generating undetected variants becomes computationally trivial, while defenders must bear the burden of behavioral analysis for every potential threat.

QUIETVAULT demonstrates that the threat extends beyond cloud-based AI services to encompass locally deployed tools. The growing adoption of local LLM frameworks creates new attack surfaces that defenders must consider. Malware that leverages the victim's own AI tools for reconnaissance generates minimal observable network traffic during the analysis phase, complicating detection efforts that rely on network monitoring.

Perhaps most significantly, these developments democratize advanced attack capabilities. Sophisticated techniques that previously required specialized expertise—polymorphic code generation, adaptive reconnaissance, intelligent credential discovery—can now be implemented by adversaries with limited technical skills simply by crafting appropriate prompts. This democratization benefits both state-sponsored actors seeking operational flexibility and financially motivated criminals looking to scale their operations.

Effective defense against these emerging threats requires organizations to fundamentally reconsider their security architectures. Traditional signature-based detection must be supplemented—and in some cases replaced—by behavioral monitoring that can identify malicious patterns regardless of surface-level code characteristics. API egress controls should be implemented to monitor and restrict communications with AI service providers from unexpected hosts. Local AI tool deployments must be treated as security-relevant applications requiring monitoring and access controls. Most importantly, security teams must develop institutional knowledge about AI-enabled attack techniques, ensuring that defenders understand how these tools can be weaponized and what defensive indicators to prioritize.

The misuse of legitimate AI tools provides advanced capabilities to actors across the threat spectrum, from nation-state espionage services to criminal enterprises. As these techniques mature and proliferate, the organizations best positioned to defend themselves will be those that recognize the fundamental nature of this shift and adapt their security postures accordingly.
